# AtmosFlow White Paper — Problem and Market

## The Problem

Indoor air quality investigations are one of the most common and least standardized activities in environmental health and safety consulting. The field lacks:

**No universal assessment protocol.** Unlike industrial hygiene exposure assessments (which follow AIHA's well-established strategy framework), IAQ investigations vary dramatically between practitioners. Two CIHs investigating the same building may produce reports that differ in scope, methodology, scoring criteria, and recommendations — not because the science disagrees, but because there is no shared workflow.

**Manual, error-prone reporting.** Most IAQ assessments are documented in Word templates that each consultant maintains independently. Threshold comparisons are performed manually. Findings are written in prose that varies with the assessor's experience and available time. A 3-hour field walkthrough routinely requires 8–20 hours of post-field report preparation.

**No structured data.** Because assessments are documented as unstructured narratives, the data cannot be aggregated, compared across buildings, or used for trend analysis. A building owner with 50 properties has no way to compare IAQ conditions across their portfolio without commissioning 50 independent reports and manually cross-referencing them.

**Inconsistent threshold application.** IAQ assessments reference multiple standards bodies (ASHRAE, OSHA, NIOSH, EPA, WHO) with different purposes: occupational exposure limits, public health guidelines, comfort criteria, and ventilation screening benchmarks. These carry different legal and technical weight, but are frequently cited interchangeably in reports — creating defensibility risk for the assessor and confusion for the client.

## The Market

### Primary: Credentialed Industrial Hygienists

- ~10,000 active CIHs in the United States (ABIH)
- IAQ investigations represent a significant portion of consulting revenue for IH firms
- Typical billable rate: $150–$300/hour; report preparation is the largest non-billable cost center
- Pain point: time spent on reporting, not on assessment quality

### Secondary: Facility Managers and Property Managers

- ~350,000 commercial building managers in the U.S. (BOMA, IFMA)
- Growing awareness of indoor air quality driven by COVID-19, ESG reporting, and tenant expectations
- No existing tool bridges the gap between "call a consultant" and "do nothing"
- Pain point: no way to document building conditions, track complaints, or demonstrate due diligence without hiring an IH for every concern

### Market Timing

Several factors converge to create demand:

1. **Post-COVID awareness.** Building occupants expect transparency about air quality. "Is the air safe?" is a question facility managers now receive regularly.
2. **ESG and WELL certification.** Corporate sustainability programs increasingly require IAQ documentation. WELL v2 performance testing requires ongoing air quality monitoring.
3. **Litigation preparation.** Property owners face increasing liability exposure from IAQ complaints. Documented assessment history is the primary defense.
4. **ASHRAE 241-2023.** The new standard for control of infectious aerosols establishes minimum ventilation requirements tied to infection risk — creating a new compliance surface.

## Regulatory Positioning

**OSHA does not regulate indoor air quality.** There are no OSHA standards specific to IAQ in non-industrial workplaces. OSHA's general duty clause (Section 5(a)(1)) can be invoked in response to complaints, but this produces hazard letters, not citations. OSHA does not proactively inspect buildings for air quality.

AtmosFlow is positioned accordingly:
- It is a **documentation and prioritization tool**, not a compliance determination engine
- It references published standards as **benchmarks**, not as compliance thresholds
- Scores are explicitly labeled as **prioritization tools**, not compliance findings
- Every report states that it supports — but does not replace — professional judgment

This positioning is deliberate and defensible. The value proposition is not "avoid OSHA citations" (which is misleading for IAQ). The value proposition is:

1. **For IH professionals:** eliminate report preparation time while producing more consistent, defensible deliverables
2. **For facility managers:** document building conditions, track complaints, and know when to call a professional — before a complaint becomes a lawsuit
